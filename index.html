üìâ Section 1 ‚Äì Factors Affecting DEMAND

```
üîë Key Rule Before You Start:
There are TWO types of change on a demand diagram:
‚Ä¢ Movement ALONG the curve = price changes (stay on same curve)
‚Ä¢ SHIFT of the whole curve = anything else (income, taste, substitutes)

UK Beef Market ‚Äì Demand Diagram

Question 1
Starting at Point E, the price falls from P2 to P1. Which new point do we move to?

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 Ask yourself: What is changing here? ‚Üí The PRICE is changing
2 A price change = movement ALONG the curve. The curve does NOT shift.
3 We stay on D2. Price falls from P2 to P1. Find where D2 meets P1.
4 That is Point B!
Answer: We move to Point B.

Explanation: A falling price does NOT shift the demand curve. We simply move along the existing D2 curve. At price P1 on curve D2, the point is B. The demand curve stays exactly where it is ‚Äì only the price and quantity change.
Question 2
Government campaign says eating beef reduces cancer risk. Market was at Point B on D2. What effect does this have?

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 Ask yourself: What is changing? ‚Üí Consumer TASTES/PREFERENCES (not price)
2 Non-price change = the whole curve SHIFTS
3 People want MORE beef at every price ‚Üí demand increases ‚Üí curve shifts RIGHT
4 D2 shifts to D3. At same price P1, we move from Point B to Point C.
Answer: The demand curve shifts right from D2 to D3, moving from Point B to Point C.

Explanation: The campaign changes people's tastes/preferences ‚Äì they now WANT more beef. This is not a price change, so the entire curve shifts to the right (an increase in demand). At the same price P1, the new point is C on curve D3.
Question 3
Swine flu causes mass slaughter of UK pigs (not for consumption). Demand is at D2. What happens to demand for beef?

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 Ask: What is the link between pigs and beef? ‚Üí Pork is a SUBSTITUTE for beef
2 Fewer pigs ‚Üí less pork supply ‚Üí pork price RISES
3 When substitute (pork) gets more expensive ‚Üí people buy MORE beef instead
4 More demand for beef ‚Üí curve shifts RIGHT ‚Üí D2 to D3
Answer: The demand curve for beef shifts right from D2 to D3.

Explanation: Swine flu reduces pork supply ‚Üí pork price rises. Since pork is a substitute for beef, when pork becomes more expensive, consumers switch to beef. This increases demand for beef, shifting the demand curve rightward to D3.

Key word: Substitute goods = products that can replace each other (e.g. pork & beef, butter & margarine).
Question 4
Government raises income tax. Market was at Point B. What happens to demand for beef? Where does the market move?

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 Ask: What is changing? ‚Üí Consumer INCOME (less money after tax)
2 Less income = non-price change = demand curve SHIFTS
3 Ask: Is beef a normal good or inferior good? This determines direction!
4 Normal good (e.g. fillet steak) ‚Üí less income ‚Üí less demand ‚Üí curve shifts LEFT ‚Üí B to A
5 Inferior good (e.g. cheap mince) ‚Üí less income ‚Üí MORE demand ‚Üí curve shifts RIGHT ‚Üí B to C
Answer: It depends on whether beef is a normal or inferior good!

‚Ä¢ If beef is a normal good (e.g. expensive fillet steak): Higher tax = less income = less demand for beef. Curve shifts LEFT from D2 to D1. Market moves from B to A.

‚Ä¢ If beef is an inferior good (e.g. cheap mince): Higher tax = less income = people buy MORE cheap beef. Curve shifts RIGHT from D2 to D3. Market moves from B to C.

Key words: Normal good = demand rises with income. Inferior good = demand falls when income rises.

üìà Section 2 ‚Äì Factors Affecting SUPPLY

```
üîë Key Rule Before You Start:
Same logic as demand but for the SUPPLY side:
‚Ä¢ Movement ALONG the curve = price of the good changes
‚Ä¢ SHIFT of the whole curve = costs, taxes, technology, weather, etc.

UK Beef Market ‚Äì Supply Diagram

Question 1
Which movement illustrates an INCREASE in supply?
(a) B to A    (b) B to C    (c) B to D    (d) B to E

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 An INCREASE in supply means suppliers provide MORE at every price level
2 More supply = the supply curve shifts to the RIGHT
3 Look at the diagram - moving right at the same price means more quantity
4 B is on S2. Moving RIGHT at the same price P1 takes us to C on S3
Answer: (b) B to C

Explanation: An increase in supply means the supply curve shifts to the RIGHT (from S2 to S3). At the same price P1, we can now supply MORE beef ‚Äì moving from Point B to Point C. More quantity is supplied at the same price = increase in supply.
Question 2
Supply is at S2, market at Point E. Government imposes a ¬£100 tax on every animal sold for beef. What happens?

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 A tax is an EXTRA cost for farmers ‚Üí it increases their costs of production
2 Higher costs = suppliers produce LESS at every price ‚Üí supply DECREASES
3 Decrease in supply = curve shifts LEFT (from S2 to S1)
4 At the same price P2, we move from Point E to Point D
Answer: Supply shifts left from S2 to S1, moving from Point E to Point D.

Explanation: The ¬£100 tax increases the cost of production for farmers. Higher costs mean farmers are willing to supply LESS beef at every price level. This shifts the entire supply curve to the left. At price P2, the market moves from E to D.
Question 3
Give THREE examples of changes that reduce beef supply (shift left). Would a fall in beef price also shift the supply curve?

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 Think about what makes it HARDER or MORE EXPENSIVE to produce beef
2 Any increase in costs or reduction in ability to produce = supply shifts LEFT
3 For the price question: ask yourself - is a price change a SHIFT or a MOVEMENT?
Three examples that shift supply LEFT:
1. Rise in production costs ‚Äì e.g. animal feed gets more expensive
2. Unfavourable weather ‚Äì e.g. drought destroys grass for cattle
3. Changes in prices of related goods ‚Äì e.g. if lamb prices rise, farmers switch to sheep instead

Would a fall in beef price shift the supply curve?
NO! A price change causes a MOVEMENT along the supply curve ‚Äì not a shift. The curve itself does not move. Farmers simply produce less at the lower price (moving down the same curve).
Question 4
Supply is at S2. The National Beef Association runs a highly successful advertising campaign. What effect on supply?

ü™ú How to Answer This:

1 Ask: Who does advertising affect? ‚Üí It affects CONSUMERS (buyers), not producers
2 Advertising changes what people WANT to buy ‚Üí it affects DEMAND
3 Supply = what PRODUCERS are willing to sell. Advertising does not change this!
4 Therefore supply stays the SAME. The supply curve does not move.
Answer: Supply remains unchanged.

Explanation: Advertising affects DEMAND, not supply. The campaign might make consumers want more beef (shifting demand right), but it does NOT change how much farmers are willing and able to supply. The supply curve stays at S2. This is a very common trick question ‚Äì always ask yourself: does this change affect BUYERS or SELLERS?

üìã Summary ‚Äì Key Rules to Remember

```
üîë The Most Important Rule of All:
Always ask yourself first: Does this change affect BUYERS or SELLERS?
Buyers ‚Üí affects DEMAND | Sellers ‚Üí affects SUPPLY

üìâ Demand ‚Äì When Does the Curve SHIFT?

These things SHIFT the demand curve:

üí∞ Income changes ‚Äì more income = more demand for normal goods (right shift)
üëÖ Tastes/Preferences ‚Äì more popular = demand shifts right
üîÑ Price of substitutes ‚Äì substitute gets expensive = demand shifts right
üîó Price of complements ‚Äì complement gets expensive = demand shifts left
üì¢ Advertising ‚Äì successful campaign = demand shifts right
‚ö†Ô∏è This does NOT shift the demand curve:
A change in the PRICE of the good itself ‚Üí this is a MOVEMENT along the curve, not a shift!

üìà Supply ‚Äì When Does the Curve SHIFT?

üí∏ Production costs rise (e.g. taxes, wages, raw materials) ‚Üí supply shifts LEFT
üå¶Ô∏è Bad weather (for farming) ‚Üí supply shifts LEFT
‚öôÔ∏è Better technology ‚Üí supply shifts RIGHT (can produce more efficiently)
üîÑ Price of related goods in production ‚Üí if other product more profitable, farmers switch
üì¢ Advertising ‚Üí affects DEMAND not supply ‚Äì supply stays the same!
‚ö†Ô∏è This does NOT shift the supply curve:
A change in the PRICE of the good itself ‚Üí this is a MOVEMENT along the curve, not a shift!
